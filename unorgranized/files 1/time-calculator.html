Time Arithmetic

The Math Behind Time Calculations

Time uses a sexagesimal (base-60) system — not base-10 — which is why adding time values requires special carry-over logic.

Seconds → Minutes
60 seconds = 1 minute
e.g. 130s = 2m 10s
Minutes → Hours
60 minutes = 1 hour
e.g. 75m = 1h 15m
Hours → Days
24 hours = 1 day
e.g. 30h = 1d 6h

What Is a Time Calculator?

A time calculator is a free online tool that lets you calculate time online — add or subtract time values in days, hours, minutes, and seconds. It works as a time duration calculator, elapsed time calculator, time difference calculator, hours calculator, minutes calculator, add time calculator, and subtract time calculator — all in one. Unlike regular arithmetic where numbers carry over at powers of ten, time uses a sexagesimal (base-60) system — 60 seconds per minute, 60 minutes per hour, 24 hours per day. Adding 45 minutes to 1 hour 25 minutes gives 2 hours 10 minutes, not "1 hour 70 minutes" — because 60 of those 70 minutes carry over into an additional hour.

Our free online time calculator handles all carry-over automatically. Whether you need a work hours calculator for tracking billable time, a travel time calculator for trip planning, a countdown calculator for events, a time interval calculator for measuring durations, a date and time calculator for project scheduling, or a speed distance time calculator — this tool covers every use case. How to Use the Time Calculator — Step by Step

Mode 1: Time Adder (Most Popular)

The Time Adder is the most commonly used mode. It lets you combine multiple time entries — each with its own + or − operator — and returns the total time.

  1. Each row represents one time entry (a work session, a leg of a trip, a task).
  2. Click + or − on each row to choose whether to add or subtract that entry.
  3. Enter days, hours, minutes, and seconds for each row — leave any field blank to treat it as zero.
  4. Click Add Another Time Row to include more entries (as many as you need).
  5. Click Calculate Total Time to see the result — broken down into d/h/m/s plus totals.
📌 Real-World Example — Freelance Hours

A freelancer worked three sessions on Monday: 1h 45m, 2h 30m, and 0h 50m. They took a 30-minute break. Total billable time?

Enter: +1h 45m / +2h 30m / +0h 50m / −0h 30m

Result: 4 hours 35 minutes

This mode answers a different question: "If I start at a specific date and time, and add (or subtract) a set duration, what is the exact resulting date and time?"

  3. Toggle Add or Subtract depending on your direction.
  4. Enter the days, hours, minutes, and seconds to offset.
  5. Click Find Result Date & Time to get the exact ending date and time.

Mode 3: Expression Mode (Power Users)

Expression Mode lets you type a time formula directly — ideal for users who are comfortable with a text-based syntax and want to enter complex calculations in one line.

Valid examples: 1d 2h + 3h 45m - 30m 10s · 5h 30m + 2h · 90m - 15s

Time Arithmetic — Why Carry-Over Matters

The most common mistake people make when adding time manually is forgetting to carry over. Here is a breakdown of how the three carry-over boundaries work:

  • 60+ seconds → every 60 seconds becomes 1 minute (remainder stays as seconds)
  • 60+ minutes → every 60 minutes becomes 1 hour (remainder stays as minutes)
  • 24+ hours → every 24 hours becomes 1 day (remainder stays as hours)

Our calculator applies all three carry-over rules simultaneously so results are always expressed in the simplest, most readable form.

A Brief History of How Humans Measure Time

The way we measure time today traces back thousands of years. The ancient Egyptians were among the first to divide the day into 12 parts using sundials, and 12 parts of night using star patterns — giving rise to the 24-hour day. Around 127 BC, the Greek astronomer Hipparchus standardized these into equal-length hours, laying the foundation for the system we still use.

The base-60 system for minutes and seconds originated with the Babylonians, who also used 60 as the base for their mathematics due to its exceptional divisibility. The number 60 can be divided evenly by 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 10, 12, 15, 20, and 30 — making it extraordinarily practical for subdividing hours and minutes without fractions.

Today, the second is defined by the cesium atomic clock — measuring exactly 9,192,631,770 oscillations of a cesium-133 atom. This definition keeps global time accurate to within one second over millions of years.

FAQ

Common Questions

Everything you need to know about adding and subtracting time values online.

How do I add hours and minutes together correctly?

Use the Time Adder tab. Enter each time value's hours and minutes in a separate row, set each row's operator to + (Add), then click Calculate. The tool automatically handles carry-over — so 45 minutes + 25 minutes correctly becomes 1 hour 10 minutes, not 70 minutes. You never need to do the carry-over math yourself.

How do I calculate total work hours across multiple sessions?

In the Time Adder, click Add Another Time Row for each work session, enter the duration of each session, and set all operators to +. Click Calculate and the result shows your total hours and minutes. This is the most accurate way to add time for freelance invoicing, timesheet reporting, or project tracking.

How many seconds are in an hour? In a day?

One hour contains 3,600 seconds (60 minutes × 60 seconds). One full day contains 86,400 seconds (24 hours × 60 minutes × 60 seconds = 24 × 3,600). One week contains 604,800 seconds. These values are shown in the stats strip above the calculator and are always displayed in your result's "Total Seconds" field.

Can I mix addition and subtraction in one calculation?

Yes — that's one of this calculator's key improvements over basic tools. In the Time Adder, every row has its own + / − toggle. Set some rows to add and others to subtract in the same calculation. For example: total work session time (+) minus total break time (−) = net productive hours. In Expression Mode, you can mix + and − freely inline: 3h 30m + 45m - 15m 10s.

What does the Date + Time mode do differently from Time Adder?

Time Adder gives you a duration — a total time span (e.g. "4 hours 35 minutes"). Date + Time Offset gives you a specific moment in time — an exact date and clock time (e.g. "Saturday, May 3, 2026 at 08:15 AM"). Use it when you need to know when something ends or begins, not just how long it takes.

What format should I use in Expression Mode?

Type values followed immediately by their unit letter: d for days, h for hours, m for minutes, s for seconds. Separate terms with + or -. Units within a term can be in any order, though conventional order (d h m s) is recommended. Examples: 2h 30m + 45m · 1d 6h - 2h 30m · 90m + 1h 20m - 15s
